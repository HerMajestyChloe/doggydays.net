How to leash train your dog.

Is your dog getting enough exercise? Are you getting enough exercise? Well, today I’m going to help you both learn to burn a few extra calories as well as get your steps in! Dogs make excellent walking buddies. Not only will your dog make you feel safer, but they will also be encouraging and motivating you every step of the way. If you have an active dog breed, they will be eager to go on a walk every day and they will surely thank you for all the attention and exploration.

When you bring a new puppy or dog home, one of the things you should do on day five is take them for a short walk around your block. Help them get to know their new neighborhood. When you first moved in did you drive around the block to get familiar with the roads as well as see your neighbors? Well, your dog is going to want to do the same thing! Not only will this help them get comfortable with the sudden change of a new home, but it will also help them for those scary times when they get lost. I know, I know, it’s not something pet owners want to ever think about but imagine your dog gets loose one day. Wouldn’t you rather the dog be able to know its way around so it can make it back home safely? When dogs get loose the majority will make their way back before the end of the day, if they know their way around the neighborhood.

When selecting a leash for your dog, I highly advise against using it with a collar. Not only could this cause unnecessary harm to the dog’s neck but tugging and pulling could lead to choking. A harness that fits appropriately is so much better for your furry friend. Not only is it more secure, but your dog will have a hard time weaseling out of this one. Make sure the leash you get is durable, has good reviews, and can flex. The ones that wind up might be good for smaller dogs, just don’t expect it to keep a German Shepherd from snapping it without a moment’s notice.

Always make sure your dog is microchipped, has tags on them that are up to date, and has all its vaccines. The last thing you want to do is expose your dog to sickness, lose them, or get a hefty fine! If you have the money, for extra security you can get a dog equipped with a tracking chip. A lot of dog owners are shocked to find out that when a dog gets microchipped it doesn’t mean they can be tracked. Instead, all it does is show a pound information on the dog (once they receive it) and to whom they belong to.

Teaching your dog to walk on a leash isn’t as hard as you’d imagine. They actually take to it quite well; younger pups will have an easier time learning this typically. That doesn’t mean older dogs can’t learn to walk gracefully on a leash by any means. If your doggy starts to misbehave NEVER, NEVER yank, pull, or drag your dog. (This is abuse.) We have much better ways of promoting good behaviors and discouraging the bad ones. Causing your dog pain will lessen the bond between you causing a whole heap of problems.

Before taking your dog on its adventure, remember they are counting on you, the alpha, to show them where they can go. Be firm and don’t show hesitation. In public anxiety can cause people fear that greatens when out and about. Dogs can pick up on these feelings and see them as a sign of weakness. You may think you’re ready to go on your first walk, but have you started by letting your pup get used to the leash inside? The outside world is filled with hustle, bustle, and tons of distractions. Teaching your dog to get used to the leash inside or in the backyard will give them the head start they need.

We want to start by teaching your pup to love the collar or harness. Let the dog get used to wearing it for short durations. If you just brought your pup home, you want to start this process on day four. On day three harness up your pup and latch on the leash. In a room with little to no distractions, get your treats and clicker. If you have already started training (Which you should have) they should understand how the clicker works. If not check out this article and teach them to sit on command to get them used to the clicker.

Let’s get started!

Step 1: With the leash in one hand stand behind your pup. Click your clicker. When the doggy turns and starts to come toward you, walk backward. Don’t continuously walk backward, after 30 – 45 seconds of walking, reward your pet with the treat and praise. Repeat this a few times but make the sessions short

Step 2: Practice walking around your yard. Carefully watch your pet, if they look distracted or have lost interest, click your clicker and take some steps away from the pup. When they come to you, reward them with a treat and praise.

Step 3: Repeat step 2, but this time take your dog on a very short walk down the street and back.

Step 4: Repeat step 3 but go farther each day.

If your dog is pulling on the leash you want to nip this behavior as soon as possible. The bigger your dog gets, the stronger the pull will be. You can correct this behavior by freezing in place. Stand still until your dog comes back to you to see why you stopped. When the leash slacks up continue walking. Do not use treats for this part as the reward will be continuing the walk. Dogs are very intelligent. They will catch on quickly that if they pull the walk stops, and that’s the last thing they want as dogs crave adventure. Remember that you always want to maintain some slack in the leash so that if either one of you changes pace, the other isn’t pulled or dragged along.

If your dog barks or chases after things, this may be a sign of lack of exercise. Make sure that you are appropriately exercising your dog. Some breeds require many hours a day of exercise. If you have corrected this and they still are getting distracted or barking, another thing you can try is visually looking ahead for potential distractions while on your walk. If you see something up ahead that could cause a loss of control, maybe take a different path, or attempt to distract your dog. Do not let them “win” by allowing them to lunge after something or go into barking mode.
